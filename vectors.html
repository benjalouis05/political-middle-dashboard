Inside the model: the geometry of political knowledge

Qwen2.5-3B-Instruct, 58 GSS-style attitude items, residual-stream activations at every layer. Companion to the behavioral dashboard. The behavioral page showed that this model portrays political moderates as tidy centrists, while real moderates are mostly cross-pressured — extreme on different issues in opposite directions. That debate is, at bottom, about dimensionality: is political opinion one axis or many? This page asks that question about the model's own internals: is politics one direction in its activation space, or many — and which directions actually cause answers?

How the raw material is made (read this first)

Every measurement on this page starts the same way. We show the model a survey question with lettered options and the frame "A randomly selected politically liberal US adult would answer (give the single letter):", and we record two things from a single forward pass:

  • The answer: the model's probability for each option letter at the final position — its exact response distribution, no sampling.
  • The thought: the residual-stream activation (a 2,048-number vector) at that final position, captured at every one of the model's 36 layers. This vector is the model's entire working state at the moment it answers.

Doing this for 4 personas (liberal, conservative, independent, moderate) × 58 items × 37 depths gives a complete map of how the model represents "a liberal answering the gun question," "a conservative answering the abortion question," and so on. A direction is a difference between two such vectors: for example, the partisan direction for the gun item = (conservative activation) − (liberal activation) on that item. It points from "thinking like a liberal" to "thinking like a conservative" about guns, inside the model's activation space.

Depth convention: we index depth by residual-stream row R = 0…36, where row 0 is the token embedding (before any layer runs) and row R is the output of decoder layer R−1. Row 0 is degenerate by construction — the final prompt token is the same character for every persona, so all personas have identical row-0 vectors and zero directions. Charts show nulls there.

How to read these two matrices — and one honest caveat

The model matrix asks: "does the model move its internal state the same way when switching liberal→conservative on item X as on item Y?" Cosine similarity between the two item directions answers that: near 1 = same internal move. The human matrix asks the closest question real survey data can answer: "do people who give conservative answers on X also give conservative answers on Y?" — the correlation between items across 18,637 respondents.

Caveat: these are analogous, not identical, quantities (a similarity of internal directions vs a correlation of behavioral responses). The comparison is fair in one specific sense: both measure how much the item-level structure collapses onto one dimension, and both are summarized the same way — by the share of variance on the first principal component (the bars below). Model: one component carries ~79%. Humans: ~31%, with real second and third dimensions.

And by depth: the red line is the one-axis share (PC1) of the direction field at each row; teal and gray are the average within-topic and between-topic cosines. The single axis appears almost immediately (≈98% at row 2) and only slowly diversifies. Note the teal and gray lines are nearly identical everywhere — topic membership barely matters to the geometry at any depth.

What "answer-relevant fraction" means

The model produces its answer by multiplying its final activation by the unembedding vectors of the option letters A–J. Only the part of a direction that lies in the (at most 9-dimensional) span of those letter vectors can change a forced-choice answer; the other ~2,039 dimensions are invisible to this readout. The answer-relevant fraction is the share of a direction's length inside that span. The earlier steering study found the "independent" concept was ~93% outside it; the bars below show the same is true of the partisan directions (median ≈ 5% inside). A direction can carry lots of information and still barely touch the answer — most of what the model "knows" here is stored in directions the answer machinery never reads.

What the steering experiment does, exactly

Steering is a causal intervention: run the liberal-persona prompt, and at one decoder layer add c × direction to the layer's output at every position, then read the option probabilities as usual. If answers shift toward the conservative pattern, the direction causally controls the answer; the strength c scales the push. Directions are built per topic (the average of that topic's item directions — only topics with ≥3 items get one) plus one global direction (average over all 58 items).

What c means. The strength c is measured in units of the model's own persona gap: the direction is the average internal difference between the conservative and liberal personas, so c = 1 would add exactly one natural liberal→conservative gap to the model's state. c = 2 pushes twice as far as the personas actually differ, c = 8 eight times as far — an exaggerated shove used to find where intervention works at all.

What the cells mean. Each cell = steer with the row's direction, measure the average answer shift on the column's items, in scale units: 0 = no movement, 1 = a full sweep from one end of the answer scale to the other (so +0.33 means answers moved a third of the scale toward that item's conservative pole; blue/negative = toward the liberal pole). "Toward conservative" uses each item's own polarity: the sign of the model's unsteered conservative−liberal gap on that item. The buttons switch strength (c) and depth (L = decoder layer). Two depths matter: L19 (mid-network, where the representation is strong) and L29 (late, where — as tab C shows — steering actually works).

The dimensionality reading: if the model kept separate per-topic knowledge, the matrix would have a dark diagonal (each topic direction moves only its own topic). Instead, at L29 every row moves every column: one shared causal axis. Own-topic cells average 0.33 scale units, other-topic cells 0.25 — cross-topic transfer is 77% of own-topic. At L19 all effects are near zero (the causal handle does not exist there yet), which is itself a finding: don't test causality at the layer where decodability peaks.

Try it: steer one question yourself

Pick a question and a starting persona, then drag the slider to push the model's internal state along its global partisan axis — right (positive) pushes toward conservative, left (negative) pushes toward liberal. Starting from the liberal persona, positive c asks "can we turn a liberal into a conservative?"; starting from the conservative persona, negative c asks the reverse. The purple bars are the steered answer distribution; blue and red outlines are what the unsteered liberal and conservative personas actually answer, so you can see whether steering reproduces the real opposite persona or overshoots into caricature. Switch between decoder layers 19 and 29 to feel the main finding: the same push does almost nothing mid-network and works late.

What exactly happens when you move the slider

The model reads the prompt "A randomly selected politically liberal US adult would answer (give the single letter):" for your chosen question. At the chosen decoder layer, we add c × g to the layer's output, where g is the global partisan axis — the average, over all 58 items, of (conservative activation − liberal activation) — and c is the slider value, in units of that natural gap: c = 1 ≈ one model's-own liberal↔conservative difference, c = 8 = eight times farther than the personas actually sit apart. Everything else runs normally, and the bars show the resulting probabilities over the option letters (computed offline for c ∈ {−8…+8}; the slider snaps to the precomputed grid). Because g is one shared axis built from all topics, the fact that it moves answers on any single question you pick is the causal one-axis result in miniature. Watch for two other things: at layer 19 the bars barely move at any strength; and at extreme strengths the distribution can overshoot past the red reference — steering is a blunt push, not a simulation of a conservative.

Reading the depth curves below
  • Blue — probe accuracy: can a linear classifier told nothing about the items separate liberal from conservative activations on held-out items? (6-fold cross-validation over items.) It can, nearly perfectly, from the first few rows: the information is decodable almost everywhere.
  • Teal — persona separability: distance between persona clusters relative to their spread (scaled to its own maximum for display).
  • Purple — independent's off-axis fraction: the share of the independent−liberal offset that is not along the item's lib–con axis; the "independent is its own concept" signature, present at all depths.
  • Amber — steering effect: the answer shift from pushing along the global partisan direction (c = 4) at that depth, scaled to its maximum. This is the causal curve — and it is near zero until row ~20, peaking around row 30.

The twenty-row gap between the blue and amber curves is the page's second finding: decodable early, causal late. The mid-network "knows" the politics (probes read it perfectly) but interventions there barely move answers; the control surface that answers actually flow through sits in the last third of the network. This also explains, in hindsight, why the earlier independent-persona steering experiments at decoder layer 19 hit a ceiling.

Methods in one paragraph. Model: Qwen2.5-3B-Instruct, completion-format prompts, exact option-letter probability readout (no sampling). Directions: differences of persona-conditioned residual-stream activations at the final prompt token, per item, per depth row (row R = output of decoder layer R−1; row 0 = embeddings, degenerate by construction). Dimensionality: PCA on the 58 length-normalized item directions; human benchmark is the eigen-spectrum of the weighted inter-item correlation matrix, GSS 2014–2024 (analogous, not identical, quantities). Answer-relevant fraction: norm share inside the centered span of the option-letter unembeddings. Steering: add c·direction to one decoder layer's output at every position during a single forward pass, liberal source persona; shifts are oriented per item by the sign of the model's own conservative−liberal behavioral gap (items with near-zero gaps contribute noise). Units: answer positions and shifts are in scale units — 0 is the first option of an item's scale, 1 the last, so a shift of 0.33 moves the expected answer a third of the way across the scale; steering strength c is in units of the model's average internal liberal→conservative persona gap. Battery: 60 GSS-style items (2 meta items excluded), 17 topics; topic steering directions exist only for topics with ≥3 items. Code: mech_interp_scripts/m_track/vector_*.py and battery60.py; raw artifacts in results/m_track/vector_explorer/ of the llm-liar repo. Findings summary: FINDINGS.md there.
